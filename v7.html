The Feature

The Georgian
Highway

Fog for breakfast. Stone towers built for siege. A mechanic who pours you coffee and calls it charity.

By the Editor Filed from Mestia Runtime 6 min

We went in before dawn because we had been warned. Everyone you meet in Tbilisi tells you the road to Mestia is impossible in early spring, and then they offer to drive it. This is the kind of country Georgia is. It is a country that speaks in contradictions and expects you to keep up.

The headlights punched holes in the fog. The fog did not move. It resented us. We climbed for two hours and saw nothing — no signs, no houses, no other headlights. Just the road, and the part of the road that had stopped being a road somewhere around four in the morning.

Cut

The towers are the first thing. They dot the landscape like headstones that decided to stay standing. Stone. Squat. Unapologetic. Built for enemies who were specific and persistent. You do not build a tower like that for an idea. You build it because somebody, at some point, was coming up that road with a grievance.

A man in Ushguli once told me his great-grandfather had bricked up a window in 1922 and nobody had reopened it. I asked why. He looked at me the way you look at a child who has asked why the sky is blue. "Because," he said, "nobody has needed to."

You don't bring maps to Svaneti. You bring a full tank, a full pack of cigarettes, and a willingness to lie about your appetite. A shepherd, near Adishi

The coffee was terrible and free. The croissant was terrible and twelve lari. Somewhere in that transaction, the economics of the entire republic made a kind of sense to me. You pay for the mountain the butter crossed, not the butter.

The road worsened, the way roads do past a certain altitude. Concrete. Then gravel. Then theory. The rental agreement had assumed pavement. The rental agreement had been unread.

A cow stood in the middle of the track for a long time. We sat in the car and watched it. It watched us back. Nobody spoke. After a while it walked off. There was no larger lesson. Sometimes a cow is just a cow, and that is the lesson.

Some roads want you on them. Svaneti is not one of those roads. Svaneti tolerates you. Barely. Field Note, third attempt

The clouds came down at five in the afternoon, the way they always do in these mountains, and the temperature fell twenty degrees in an hour. I went inside. I ordered a vodka. The mechanic was there. He raised his glass. He said nothing. There was nothing to say.

Outside, the fog came back for the road. The road did not argue.

Fin.
