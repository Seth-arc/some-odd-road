One

I'll tell you about Georgia. Not the state — the country. People hear "Georgia" and think you mean Atlanta, which is almost never what I mean. I mean the one with the mountains and the vodka and the towers somebody built for a specific enemy I will never meet.

We went in before dawn. I don't remember whose idea that was — probably mine, I'm the kind of person who decides things like that and then acts surprised when nobody else is thrilled about it. The fog was everywhere. It sat on the valley like it owned the valley, which maybe it did.

she asked me if i was scared. i said no. i was scared.

The thing about Georgian drivers is they lie to you on purpose. Every one of them. In Tbilisi they say the road to Mestia is impossible in spring, and then they offer to drive it. Everyone offers. That's the whole thing about this country. It's a country of offers you shouldn't accept, and then do.

The headlights punched holes in the fog. The fog did not move. It resented us. We climbed for two hours and saw nothing. No signs. No houses. No other headlights. Just the road, and the part of the road that had stopped being a road somewhere around four in the morning. And that's when I started laughing, and couldn't stop, and didn't really try.

Two
cut this? no, keep it. the towers are the whole thing.

The towers are the part nobody tells you about. Or they tell you, but they get it wrong. They say "defensive architecture" the way you'd say a color or a decade, like it's a style. It isn't a style. It's a history. Every tower was built because somebody — specific, named, still remembered — was coming up that road with a grievance. And you can tell the Georgians are still a little proud of it.

"See that one?" the man in Ushguli said. "That was my great-grandfather's. He bricked up a window in 1922." I asked why. He looked at me the way you look at a child. He said, "Because nobody has needed to reopen it."

That is the truest sentence anybody has said to me all year. I wrote it down in the back of my passport in case I forgot. I forget a lot of things now. I don't forget sentences like that.

You pay for the mountain the butter crossed, not the butter. A shepherd, near Adishi

Coffee was free if you knew the mechanic. Twelve lari if you didn't. The croissant was yesterday's. The croissant was twelve lari anyway. I ate it. I drank the coffee. I paid for everything, and then I left a tip that was larger than the bill, which is the sort of thing you do when you are trying to performapologize for being foreign. It was the best twelve lari I have ever spent and I will not be taking questions about it.

Three
Plate II — The mechanic, probably, or somebody just like him.

The road fell apart in stages. Concrete. Gravel. Theory. Somewhere in the gravel I laughed again, this time alone — I don't know why. The cow was already gone. The cow had been gone for half an hour. I had been driving and not driving and sitting still and moving, all at the same time, the way you do when you are no longer certain where the road is, only that it has not given up on you yet.

The clouds came down at five. They always do. You learn that fast or you don't learn it. I went inside. I ordered a vodka. The mechanic was there. He raised his glass. Neither of us said anything. He knew. I knew. It didn't need to be said, and we didn't say it, and outside the fog came back for the road, and the road did not argue.

— s.
