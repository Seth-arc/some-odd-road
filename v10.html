Some Odd Road No. 043 · The Walk
An exhibition, in seven parts

The Walk

Before the sun, through a town that hasn't yet decided to wake up.

Mestia, Georgia · 42.7167° N · 42.7500° E · 04:42 — 06:34 · 1.9 km
The route · Seven waypoints
I. Svaneti Street, outside the guesthouse 04:42 · 0.0 km

I left the guesthouse at four forty-two, which is the kind of time you only leave places when you are running from something, or trying to prove you are not. I was not running from anything. The mechanic let me out. He did not wish me well. This is Georgia.

II. Orbeliani Lane 04:51 · 0.3 km

The dogs here work a night shift. I walked past three of them; only one bothered to open an eye. The other two had seen worse. Mestia dogs are the last functional union in the republic, and they enforce an eleven-minute rule I do not understand but have decided to respect.

III. Setisjvari Church, the locked one 05:04 · 0.6 km

The church was locked, which I considered fair. Nobody should have to be open at five in the morning. I stood outside for eleven minutes and thought of nothing in particular. A man walked past carrying a shovel. He nodded. I nodded. Neither of us had any business with the shovel.

IV. Mestia Bakery (not yet open) 05:18 · 0.8 km

There is a specific smell to Mestia in the hour before the sun comes up, which is coal smoke and cold wool and something else I will not identify in case it turns out to be a person. The bakery was cold. A single bulb hung over the door, doing its best. The bread would arrive in ninety minutes. It would be worth the wait. I did not wait.

V. The Square (no square, really) 05:33 · 1.1 km

What locals call the square is a quadrilateral that slopes to the left, on which three benches have been arranged at no discernible angle. A cat owns the middle bench. A man owns the third. Nobody owns the first. The first bench is where I sat.

VI. Zagari Road, north-east edge 05:58 · 1.6 km

The road out of town is unmarked. You know it by the fact that it stops being interesting. I walked until I could see a tower on the ridge. I stopped. Walking toward a tower at dawn is something people have been doing here for nine hundred years, and those people mostly did not make it back. I turned around.

VII. The Guesthouse Door, return 06:34 · 1.9 km

The mechanic was still up. He had been up the whole time. I think he had been up for several days. He asked me how the walk was. I said it was fine. He asked if I was hungry. I said no. He poured me a small glass of something that was not vodka, technically, and we sat and did not discuss it. The sun came up around six twenty-one. Neither of us mentioned it.
