The Georgian Highway

A fog. A mechanic. A road that was not a road.

By the Editor Filed from Mestia 6 min

Mestia, GeorgiaWe went in before dawn because we had been warned. Everyone you meet in Tbilisi tells you the road to Mestia is impossible in early spring, and then they offer to drive it.

The headlights punched holes in the fog. The fog did not move. It resented us. We climbed for two hours and saw nothing. No signs. No houses. No other headlights. Just the road, and the part of the road that had stopped being a road somewhere around four in the morning.

The towers are the first thing. Stone. Squat. Built for enemies who were specific and persistent. You do not build a tower like that for an idea. You build it because somebody, at some point, was coming up that road with a grievance.

You don't bring maps to Svaneti. You bring a full tank and a willingness to lie about your appetite.

The coffee was terrible and free. The croissant was twelve lari. Somewhere in that transaction, the economics of the entire republic made a kind of sense. You pay for the mountain the butter crossed, not the butter.

The road worsened, the way roads do past a certain altitude. Concrete. Then gravel. Then theory. The rental agreement had assumed pavement. The rental agreement had been unread.

The clouds came down at five. I went inside. I ordered a vodka. The mechanic was there. He raised his glass. He said nothing. There was nothing to say.
