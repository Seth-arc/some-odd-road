22 April 2026 Svaneti, Georgia 6 min read Dispatch No. 042

The Georgian Highway

The road into Upper Svaneti — somewhere between infrastructure and memory.

The air here tastes like rusted iron and old snow. We crossed into the valley just before dawn, the headlights cutting through a thick, obstinate fog that seemed to resent our intrusion. This isn't the sort of place you end up in by accident; it requires a deliberate sequence of poor navigational choices, a disregard for road-surface warnings, and the kind of optimism about fuel gauges that borders on theology.

To understand the local temperament, you must look at the architecture. The towers dot the landscape like stone sentinels — squat, humourless things built not for aesthetics but out of absolute, paranoid necessity. The kind of architecture that assumes history is cyclical, and the next cycle involves someone trying to burn your house down. In Svaneti, this assumption has proven correct often enough to justify the investment.

"The geography dictates the commerce. Here, you pay for the logistics of flour moving up a mountain, not the butter."

A black coffee costs you nothing if you know the right mechanic, or about 12 GEL if you decide to sit at the only café with a working espresso machine. The croissant is an entirely different negotiation.

We drove further up the ridge. The concept of a "road" became increasingly abstract, morphing from broken concrete to packed dirt, and finally to a suggestion of a path worn into the rock by generations of stubborn cattle. The rental agreement said nothing about this. The rental agreement assumed you would remain on surfaces that had been paved at some point in the post-Soviet era.

The map shows a track. The locals say it washed out in October, leaving the infrastructure in a state of terminal disrepair. My last visit here was in 2018. The roads are a fraction better, but the isolation remains absolute. Cell service is a rumour. Wi-Fi is a philosophical concept. The only reliable signal is the one the mountains send every evening when the clouds descend and the temperature drops twenty degrees in an hour: go inside, or become a cautionary anecdote.
